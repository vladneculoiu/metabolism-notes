# 04 -- Protein

> Why it is not stored, how it protects muscle, and why consistent intake is mandatory.

[Fats](03-fats.md) | [Insulin](05-insulin.md)

---

## The full path

```text
1. YOU EAT (meat, eggs, fish, dairy, protein shake)
        
2. DIGESTION (proteolysis) -> AMINO ACIDS
        
3. They reach the LIVER (the manager) -> distributed through the body
        
4. They enter the "amino acid POOL" (small, ~100g, in blood + cells)
        
5. Amino acids go in several directions, by priority:
        ---- PROTEIN SYNTHESIS -> muscle/organs  priority
        ---- Enzymes, hormones, antibodies, immune system
        ---- Energy if needed -> gluconeogenesis -> ATP
        ---- Large surplus -> DEAMINATION -> nitrogen -> urea (urine) + remainder -> burned/fat
        
6. RISK if the pool is empty + you do not eat protein:
        ---- MUSCLE PROTEOLYSIS -> the body breaks down muscle -> amino acids
```

---

## The big particularity: protein is not stored

The most important difference from the other macronutrients:

```text
Carbohydrates -> glycogen (~500g store)           storage
Fats          -> adipocytes (~unlimited store)    storage
Protein       -> no dedicated storage            no reservoir
```

### The amino acid pool: small and temporary

The "pool" is not an organ. It is the sum of **free** amino acids in the blood and inside cells, managed by the liver:

- **Size:** ~100g free amino acids total, tiny compared with ~10-12kg structural protein in muscle/organs
- **Duration:** empties within a few hours after a meal
- **Locations:** blood plasma (small, ~5-6g) + intracellular fluid (most of it)

```text
Analogy:
   Pool = checking account (small, for daily spending)
   Muscles/organs = structural wealth (you do not want to sell it for bills)
```

---

## Why the body "takes from muscle"

```text
No protein intake + the body needs amino acids
        
The small pool empties within hours
        
There is no reserve storage
        
The only large source = muscle (structural protein)
        
MUSCLE PROTEOLYSIS = catabolism
```

> **Solution:** dietary amino acids before the pool empties. This is why **consistent intake during the day** matters more than a single total number.

---

## Distribution matters more than the total

The body efficiently uses about **~25-40g protein per meal**:

```text
 4 meals x 30g protein = 120g distributed
   -> pool stays filled -> muscle protected

 1 meal x 120g protein = wasteful
   -> surplus is not stored -> broken down/eliminated
   -> the pool is empty for the rest of the day -> catabolism risk
```

---

## Can protein turn into fat?

**Theoretically yes, practically negligible.**

Pathway: amino acids -> deamination (remove nitrogen) -> carbon skeleton -> lipogenesis -> fat.

Why it is rare:

1. **Energetically expensive**: the body pays a high cost and avoids it
2. **Higher priorities:** muscle, enzymes, direct energy, glucose all come first
3. **High thermic effect:** you burn ~25-30% of protein calories just digesting them
4. **Strong satiety:** it is hard to eat a huge protein surplus

> **Practical conclusion:** protein does not make you fat. A **total calorie surplus** does, and that surplus almost always comes from refined carbs + fats, not protein.

---

## Protein and insulin
Protein raises insulin **moderately**: less than carbohydrates, but not zero.

```text
Carbohydrates -> strong insulin rise, glucagon unchanged -> storage
Protein       -> moderate insulin rise + glucagon rise    -> balance
```

Glucagon partly offsets the storage effect, so fat is not locked away as strongly. This is why a protein snack is much less harmful than a sugary snack, although it still interrupts the burning window.

---

## When muscle catabolism appears

| Situation | Muscle proteolysis risk |
|---|---|
| Rest, enough protein | Minimal (~2-5% of energy) |
| Intense effort + **full** glycogen | Low, glycogen covers it |
| Intense effort + **empty** glycogen | **High**, body attacks muscle |
| Long fast + insufficient protein | **High** |
| Aggressive deficit + low protein | **High** |

> **Paradox:** rest is "safer" for muscle than intense fasted effort. Light/moderate fasted activity burns fat; intense fasted effort can attack muscle.

---

## Particularities

- **Not stored**: no reservoir, so intake must be consistent during the day
- Raises insulin **moderately** + raises glucagon, a better balance than carbs
- The most **satiating** macronutrient
- Highest **thermic effect**: you burn calories just digesting it (~25-30%)
- Energy: **4 kcal/g**
- Recomposition target: **2-2.2g/kg body weight/day**, split across 3-4 meals
